What I actually do when I don't have my Instrument

I left my Instrument back at home. This was the practical decision — tablas are fragile, travel is hard on instruments, I didn't know how long I'd be here — but it's also the decision I regret most about moving to Bloomington. It turns out I use tabla more as a thinking tool than I'd realized when it was available to me every evening.

So here is what I actually do. I play on the railing of my balcony. The railing is wooden, pretty flat, and produces a surprisingly satisfying thunk when you tap on it. It's not a tabla. It has no resonance, no tonal variation of any kind, the syllables that give tabla its vocabulary. But it has surface area, and it has a fixed position in space, and that's enough to count.

What I actually do, is practicing technique and control. It's more like keeping the rhythm alive in my hands. Tabla players talk about the way a rhythm gets into your body, the way you stop counting and start feeling the cycle turn. You can lose that, I think, if you leave it alone for too long. The railing is how I keep it from going cold.

There are other things I do other than actively practicing. I listen to recordings from various artists, across all genres. I love to count in my head while I'm walking, feeling for where the 'sam' would fall. Sam is the first beat, the beat everything resolves to, the beat that gives the cycle its shape. When I'm walking and counting, I'm really just asking: where does this moment land in the cycle? It's a strange thing to carry around, but it makes the world feel less random.

I don't know when I'll have my tabla again. Probably not until I go back to India, which feels both close and very far. Until then, the railing, the headphones, the walking. It's enough to keep the thread.
